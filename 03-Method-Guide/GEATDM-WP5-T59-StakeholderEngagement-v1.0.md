# 1. INTRODUCTION

## 1.1 Purpose

This document sets out the method by which a GEATDM practitioner identifies, classifies, engages, and sustains the support of stakeholders across a public-sector EA programme. It addresses two operating constraints set out in the Public Sector Reality framing (T15):

- **Political sponsorship** (PAERA §3.1.1) — substantial changes need political support expressed in words and budget allocations.
- **Capacity** (PAERA §3.1, §4.1.1) — the workforce shape of public-sector ministries forces differentiated engagement approaches per ministry.

The method is grounded in field experience from public-sector EA programmes and generalised for any country context. A worked example with named ministries appears in §3.5.

## 1.2 Scope

This document covers the engagement of ministries, agencies, oversight bodies, and parliamentary committees within a national government. It does not cover external stakeholders (citizens, business associations, civil society), which are addressed in PAERA §3.3.3 (Driving Adoption) and in sectoral guides.

# 2. THE THREE-TIER STAKEHOLDER MODEL

## 2.1 Model overview

GEATDM classifies ministry-level stakeholders into three tiers, each with a distinct engagement approach. The classification is based on the role each ministry plays in the national digital transformation, not on its substantive sector.

| Tier | Role | Engagement intensity | Decision authority | Indicative duration |
|------|------|---------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Tier 1 — Champions** | Transformation catalysts. Decision-making authority over budgets and IT investment. Integration hubs. | Weekly workshops, embedded technical support. | High — drive cross-government decisions. | 9-month engagement aligned with full GEATDM cycle. |
| **Tier 2 — Early Adopters** | Service-delivery organisations ready for active transformation. | Hands-on support, dedicated focal points. | Medium — adopt and operationalise. | 6-month assessment phase. |
| **Tier 3 — Observers** | Other ministries preparing for future implementation. | Monthly briefings, structured observation. | Low — learn and prepare. | 18-month preparation period. |

## 2.2 Tier 1 — Champions

Tier 1 Champions typically include three to four ministries with cross-government authority:

- **The Finance Ministry** — controls government-wide budgets, hosts the Government Financial Management Information System (GFMIS), runs the Treasury Single Account, and operates the procurement workflow. The ministry's control over budgets provides the leverage that makes EA adoption enforceable.
- **The ICT Ministry / Digital Authority** — owns the government enterprise architecture, the digital identity system, the cybersecurity infrastructure, and the government service bus architecture. This ministry's existing integration patterns form the foundation for any EA implementation.
- **A high-volume Service Delivery Authority** — typically Health, Tax, or Customs depending on the country. This ministry is included in Tier 1 because its operational scale generates the data flows and integration patterns that the rest of government must accommodate.
- **Optionally the Ministry of Justice or Cabinet Office** where legal-framework coordination requires direct sponsorship.

Champion ministries receive intensive engagement: weekly workshops during DISCOVER and ASSESS, embedded technical advisors during ADAPT, and direct involvement in the EA Governance Board through EXECUTE & GOVERN.

Information gathering for Champion ministries captures their unique governance responsibilities — budget control mechanisms that enforce EA compliance, technology standardisation authority, coordination of multi-ministry initiatives — and their integration-hub functions: all inter-ministry data flows, shared service responsibilities, citizen service delivery touchpoints.

## 2.3 Tier 2 — Early Adopters

Tier 2 Early Adopters typically include two to four ministries that are ready for active transformation but operate at sectoral rather than cross-government scale. Common Tier 2 candidates: Interior, Education, Agriculture, Social Protection.

Early Adopter ministries receive hands-on support to:

- Document operational processes and pain points.
- Map citizen service journeys end-to-end.
- Identify opportunities for integration with central ministries.
- Operationalise the Reference Architecture for their sector.

Early Adopter information gathering focuses on operational assessments capturing current service delivery models, sector-specific compliance requirements, change-management capacity, and previous technology project experience. Their high public visibility demands careful documentation of staff technical capabilities, organisational culture, and project history.

The 6-month assessment phase for Early Adopters runs concurrently with the Champion ministries' Wave 1 implementation and feeds into the Champions' integration architecture as it solidifies.

## 2.4 Tier 3 — Observers

Tier 3 Observers comprise the remaining ministries. Engagement is light-touch and educational. Observers receive:

- Monthly briefings on the EA programme and the Reference Architecture progress.
- Structured access to Early Adopter experience through community-of-practice sessions.
- Progressive skill-development workshops over an 18-month preparation period.
- Self-paced access to GEATDM materials.

Observer ministries are not asked for active commitment during the engagement. They are asked to be ready to begin their own DISCOVER phase 12 to 18 months after the original programme starts. This staged approach respects the limited capacity of the central digital authority to provide hands-on support to all ministries simultaneously.

## 2.5 Hybrid and special cases

Some ministries do not fit cleanly into one tier. The classification follows three rules:

1. **Operational scale dominates.** A ministry that operates a high-volume, citizen-facing service is Tier 1 or Tier 2 even if its sectoral mandate is narrow. A ministry that operates only a small number of internal processes is Tier 3 even if its policy importance is high.
2. **Integration role dominates.** A ministry that is an integration hub for the rest of government (Finance, ICT) is always Tier 1.
3. **Political weight modifies but does not override.** A ministry headed by a senior minister can be elevated one tier (Tier 3 → Tier 2, or Tier 2 → Tier 1) but cannot be elevated two tiers (Tier 3 → Tier 1) without sustained capacity investment.

The Toolkit template TK-33 (Stakeholder Tier Classification) provides the questionnaire and decision tree for systematic classification.

# 3. THE PIC MATRIX — POWER, INTEREST, CULTURE

## 3.1 Why three dimensions

Standard stakeholder analysis uses a two-dimensional Power-Interest matrix. For public-sector EA work, two dimensions are insufficient. A third dimension — Culture — is needed because two ministries with identical Power and Interest scores can have radically different engagement readiness depending on their organisational culture, change history, and innovation appetite.

## 3.2 The three dimensions

**Power** — the ministry's decision-making authority over outcomes that matter to the EA programme:

- Budget control (own budget; ability to commit funds to integration work).
- Veto capability (can the ministry refuse to participate in a cross-government initiative).
- Standards-setting authority (can the ministry impose technical standards on others).
- Delivery authority (can the ministry deliver outcomes that the rest of government depends on).

**Interest** — the ministry's stake in the EA outcome:

- Direct benefit from the EA (does the ministry's mission depend on the integration that EA enables).
- Operational impact (does the EA change daily working practices in the ministry).
- Strategic alignment (does the EA support the ministry's published strategy).

**Culture** — the ministry's readiness to engage:

- Organisational readiness (existing governance, project management capacity, vendor relationships).
- Innovation appetite (history of adopting new technology, willingness to experiment).
- Change history (track record of previous transformations: success, mixed, failure).
- Leadership posture (current leadership's stance on digital transformation).

## 3.3 Scoring

Each dimension is scored on a five-point scale (1 = low, 5 = high) using the rubric in TK-34 PIC Matrix Worksheet. Scores are based on documentary evidence (annual reports, audit findings, previous-project reviews) and stakeholder interviews, not on impressions.

## 3.4 Reading the matrix

The PIC Matrix produces a triple (P, I, C) for each ministry. The classification rules:

| Profile | Engagement implication |
|---------|----------------------|
| High P, High I, High C | Champion — full engagement, expect leadership. |
| High P, High I, Low C | Champion in mandate but not yet ready — invest in cultural readiness before deep engagement. |
| High P, Low I, * | Champion in mandate but not engaged — use formal channels (cabinet, finance committee) to elevate Interest. |
| Low P, High I, High C | Early Adopter — operationalise quickly, use as a demonstration case for the rest. |
| Low P, High I, Low C | Early Adopter at risk — invest in capacity-building before relying on delivery. |
| Low P, Low I, * | Observer — light-touch engagement, no near-term delivery dependency. |

## 3.5 Worked example — Gambia 2025

The Gambia National EA Programme (June 2025 – February 2026) classified six ministries using the PIC Matrix. The published classification:

| Ministry | Power | Interest | Culture | Tier |
|----------|-------|----------|---------|------|
| Ministry of Finance and Economic Affairs (MoFEA) | 5 | 4 | 3 | Tier 1 — Champion |
| Ministry of Communications and Digital Economy (MoCDE) | 4 | 5 | 4 | Tier 1 — Champion |
| Ministry of Health (MoH) | 3 | 4 | 3 | Tier 1 — Champion (operational scale) |
| Gambia Revenue Authority (GRA) | 3 | 4 | 4 | Tier 1 — Champion (operational scale) |
| Customs (within GRA) | 3 | 4 | 4 | Tier 2 — Early Adopter |
| Ministry of the Interior | 3 | 3 | 2 | Tier 2 — Early Adopter |
| Ministry of Education | 3 | 3 | 3 | Tier 2 — Early Adopter |

The classification is published with the rationale for each score in the Gambia Stakeholder Analysis & Engagement Plan. Other ministries (Agriculture, Justice, Trade, Local Government, Foreign Affairs, etc.) were classified as Tier 3 Observers and engaged through monthly briefings.

# 4. ENGAGEMENT READINESS ASSESSMENT

## 4.1 Purpose

The PIC Matrix tells the practitioner how a ministry should be engaged. The Engagement Readiness Assessment (ERA) tells the practitioner whether the ministry is ready to be engaged at all, and where capacity-building is needed before engagement can be productive.

## 4.2 Three dimensions of readiness

**Technical capability** — current IT maturity:

- Existence of an internal IT function with documented architecture.
- Operational digital systems (registries, transactions, integrations).
- Use of modern development practices (version control, CI/CD, cloud-native deployment).
- Current vendor and contractor landscape.

**Human capacity** — available skilled staff:

- Permanent staff with relevant skills (architects, engineers, data professionals).
- Capacity to allocate staff time to the EA programme without operational disruption.
- Recent and planned recruitment for relevant roles.
- Training and development pipeline.

**Leadership commitment** — demonstrated through actions:

- Senior leadership has signed the Stakeholder Commitment Letter (or equivalent).
- The ministry has appointed a named focal point with explicit time allocation.
- Budget has been earmarked or applied for.
- Internal communication has been issued announcing the engagement.

## 4.3 Readiness levels

Each dimension is scored Green / Yellow / Red:

- **Green.** Proceed with planned engagement. The ministry is ready.
- **Yellow.** Proceed with caution. Capacity-building activities should run in parallel with engagement. Adjust the engagement timeline to reflect the capacity-building need.
- **Red.** Do not proceed with substantive engagement. Capacity-building must precede engagement. The ministry remains in Tier 3 (Observer) until at least one dimension moves to Yellow.

Where any dimension is Red, the practitioner reports back to the EA Programme governance with a remediation plan and a revised timeline. Proceeding with substantive engagement against a Red readiness is the most common cause of failed EA programmes.

## 4.4 ERA refresh cadence

The Engagement Readiness Assessment is performed:

- At the start of DISCOVER for every ministry in the engagement scope.
- At the start of each subsequent phase, with a focus on ministries that were Yellow at the previous assessment.
- Quarterly thereafter, as part of the EA Board cadence.

ERA results are visible to the EA Board and to the ministry's own leadership. The transparency creates accountability and surfaces capacity-building needs early.

# 5. TIER-SPECIFIC ENGAGEMENT STRATEGY

## 5.1 Tier 1 — Champion engagement strategy

| Activity | Cadence | Format | Output |
|----------|---------|--------|--------|
| **Tier 1 Inception Workshop** | Once, at start | One-day hybrid (in-person + virtual ITU facilitation) | Signed Stakeholder Commitment Letters; designated focal points; communication matrix; identified quick wins. |
| **Weekly working sessions** | Weekly throughout DISCOVER and ASSESS | 90-minute hybrid sessions | Structured information gathering; decision points; risk surfacing. |
| **Bi-weekly Steering Committee** | Bi-weekly | One-hour sponsor-level meeting | Sponsorship maintenance; cross-cutting decisions; political escalation. |
| **EA Board membership** | Permanent | Monthly EA Board meetings | Governance, compliance, dispensations. |
| **Embedded technical support** | Continuous during ADAPT | On-site or remote architect | Tailoring decisions; AS-IS-to-TO-BE mapping; sector-specific extensions. |
| **Quarterly Cabinet briefing** | Quarterly | Written brief plus minister-level meeting | Political sponsorship maintenance; budget alignment; visible quick wins. |

The Tier 1 Inception Workshop deserves special treatment. Its one-day agenda, drawn from the Gambia precedent (TK-35 Tier 1 Inception Workshop Concept Note), includes:

- **9:00 — 10:30** Background, scope, methodology, timeline. Anchor on the country's existing Digital Master Plan or equivalent.
- **10:45 — 12:30** Tier 1 ministry presentations: each Champion ministry presents its current state, priorities, and concerns (15 minutes each).
- **13:30 — 15:00** Working session: identify quick wins, communication matrix, focal points.
- **15:00 — 16:00** Closing: signing of Stakeholder Commitment Letters; agreement on next steps; press communiqué (where appropriate).

The workshop is hybrid because senior officials' availability for full-day in-person sessions is limited. ITU or partner facilitation is provided virtually so the country team can focus on substantive engagement.

## 5.2 Tier 2 — Early Adopter engagement strategy

| Activity | Cadence | Format | Output |
|----------|---------|--------|--------|
| **Tier 2 Inception Briefing** | Once, after Tier 1 inception | Half-day per ministry | Awareness; designated focal point; agreed engagement schedule. |
| **Bi-weekly working sessions** | Bi-weekly | 90-minute hybrid sessions | AS-IS documentation; pain-point identification; sector-specific tailoring. |
| **Sector workshop** | Once, mid-engagement | One-day workshop with multiple Tier 2 ministries | Cross-sector pattern identification; shared service opportunities. |
| **Monthly progress reporting** | Monthly | Written progress report | Status, blockers, decisions required. |
| **EA Board observer status** | Monthly | EA Board attendance without voting rights | Awareness of governance decisions affecting the ministry. |

Tier 2 ministries provide written progress reports rather than attending bi-weekly Steering Committee. The Steering Committee receives a consolidated Tier 2 report from the central digital authority.

## 5.3 Tier 3 — Observer engagement strategy

| Activity | Cadence | Format | Output |
|----------|---------|--------|--------|
| **Programme Launch Briefing** | Once, at programme start | Half-day all-Observer briefing | Awareness; understanding of programme objectives. |
| **Monthly bulletin** | Monthly | Written bulletin (one page) | Programme status; learning from Champion and Early Adopter experience; upcoming opportunities to engage. |
| **Quarterly community-of-practice session** | Quarterly | Half-day session with rotating ministry presentations | Knowledge transfer; preparation for future engagement; relationship-building. |
| **Self-paced access to GEATDM materials** | Continuous | Online repository with documents and templates | Independent capacity-building. |
| **Pre-engagement readiness review** | At month 12 and month 18 | ERA refresh per ministry | Identification of ministries ready to move to Tier 2 or Tier 1 in a follow-on engagement. |

The Tier 3 cadence is calibrated so that Observer ministries are prepared, when their turn comes, to begin their own DISCOVER phase without significant onboarding cost.

## 5.4 Cross-tier activities

Some activities span all three tiers:

- **EA Programme website or repository** — single point of truth for programme materials, accessible to all tiers.
- **Annual EA Conference** — year-end conference where Champions present results, Early Adopters present plans, Observers learn. Builds the community of practice and demonstrates programme value to political sponsors.
- **Inter-ministerial communication channel** — formal channel (typically through the central digital authority) for ministry-level escalation.

# 6. COMMUNICATION PLAN

## 6.1 Communication objectives

Communication objectives differ by tier and by audience within each tier:

| Audience | Tier 1 | Tier 2 | Tier 3 |
|----------|--------|--------|--------|
| Minister / political head | Sponsorship maintenance, quarterly briefings | Awareness, mid-engagement briefing | Awareness, annual briefing |
| Permanent Secretary / DG | Engagement leadership, weekly updates | Operational oversight, monthly | Awareness, quarterly |
| ICT Director / CIO | Working partnership, weekly | Working partnership, bi-weekly | Awareness, monthly |
| Focal point | Daily engagement | Bi-weekly engagement | Monthly engagement |
| Operational staff | Workshop participation | Workshop participation | Self-paced access |

## 6.2 Communication channels

| Channel | Purpose | Frequency |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| Steering Committee minutes | Governance decisions and political-sponsorship record | Bi-weekly |
| EA Board minutes | Architecture decisions and dispensation record | Monthly |
| Programme bulletin | Programme status across all tiers | Monthly |
| Ministry-specific brief | Tier-1 and Tier-2 ministry-specific updates | Monthly |
| Public website / repository | All-stakeholder transparency | Continuous |
| Press briefings | External communication | At milestones |

## 6.3 Communication products

The communication plan produces a set of standard products with consistent format and tone:

- **Briefing note** — two pages, structured (situation / decision required / options / recommendation). Used for Steering Committee and Cabinet briefings.
- **Status report** — four to six pages, structured (executive summary / progress by Wave / risks / decisions required / next steps).
- **Architecture decision record (ADR)** — one page per decision, structured (context / decision / consequences). Used by the EA Board.
- **Dispensation request** — two pages, structured (deviation requested / rationale / impact / proposed mitigation). Used to manage exceptions to the Reference Architecture.
- **Press communiqué** — one page, structured (announcement / context / impact / next steps). Used at programme milestones.

Templates are provided in the Toolkit (TK-23 Dispensation Request, TK-24 EA Status Report, TK-30 Architecture Decision Record).

## 6.4 Tone and posture

GEATDM communication maintains a measured tone. Reasons:

- Public-sector audiences are sceptical of marketing-style language. Plain declarative sentences carry more weight.
- Political sponsors need to be able to use the materials in their own communications. Materials written in consultant voice cannot be reused.
- Architects who succeed each other on the programme need to read previous decisions clearly. Hedge-and-qualify language obscures what was decided.

Quantitative claims (timelines, costs, capacity needs) are stated with explicit assumptions and the conditions under which they hold. Risks are stated as risks, not as opportunities.

# 7. ENGAGEMENT TIMELINE BY GEATDM PHASE

## 7.1 DISCOVER phase engagement activities

| Week | Activity | Tier |
|------|----------|------|
| 1 | Programme Launch Briefing for all ministries | All |
| 2 | Tier 1 Inception Workshop | Tier 1 |
| 3 | Tier 2 Inception Briefings (one per ministry) | Tier 2 |
| 3–4 | Stakeholder Tier Classification (TK-33) refreshed against actual engagement | All |

| 4 | Engagement Readiness Assessment for all ministries | All |

## 7.2 ASSESS phase engagement activities

| Week | Activity | Tier |
|------|----------|------|
| 5–8 | Weekly working sessions continue | Tier 1 |
| 5–8 | Bi-weekly working sessions begin | Tier 2 |
| 6 | First Steering Committee | Tier 1 sponsors |

| 8 | First monthly bulletin to Tier 3 | Tier 3 |

## 7.3 ADAPT phase engagement activities

| Week | Activity | Tier |
|------|----------|------|
| 9–12 | Embedded technical support during tailoring | Tier 1 |
| 10–12 | Tier 2 sector tailoring workshops | Tier 2 |
| 11 | First Cabinet briefing | Tier 1 sponsors |
| 12 | First quarterly community-of-practice session | Tier 3 |

## 7.4 PLAN phase engagement activities

| Week | Activity | Tier |
|------|----------|------|
| 13–16 | Roadmap working sessions | Tier 1 |
| 13–16 | Tier 2 sector roadmap inputs | Tier 2 |
| 14 | Mid-PLAN Cabinet briefing | Tier 1 sponsors |
| 16 | First EA Board meeting (full membership) | Tier 1 + Tier 2 observers |
| 16 | Tier 3 planning session for follow-on engagement | Tier 3 |

## 7.5 EXECUTE & GOVERN phase engagement activities

| Frequency | Activity | Tier |
|-----------|----------|------|
| Weekly | Tier 1 working sessions on active Wave-1 implementation | Tier 1 |
| Bi-weekly | Steering Committee | Tier 1 sponsors |
| Monthly | EA Board | Tier 1 + Tier 2 observers |
| Monthly | Programme bulletin | All |
| Quarterly | Cabinet briefing | Tier 1 sponsors |
| Quarterly | Community-of-practice session | Tier 3 |
| Annually | EA Conference | All |

## 7.6 Engagement effort estimate

For a 9-month programme covering three Tier 1 ministries, three Tier 2 ministries, and twelve Tier 3 observers:

| Tier | Effort per ministry (person-days) | Number of ministries | Total person-days |
|------|-----------------------------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Tier 1 | 60 | 3 | 180 |
| Tier 2 | 25 | 3 | 75 |
| Tier 3 | 4 | 12 | 48 |
| Cross-tier (Steering, Cabinet, EA Board, conference) | 60 | — | 60 |
| **Total** | | | **363 person-days** |

This is the engagement effort, distinct from the technical EA work effort. Engagement effort scales linearly with the number of Tier 1 and Tier 2 ministries, sub-linearly with the number of Tier 3 ministries.

# 8. RISK MANAGEMENT FOR ENGAGEMENT

## 8.1 Common risks

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|--------|------------|
| **Loss of political sponsor** (cabinet reshuffle, election cycle) | Medium | High | Multiple sponsors across cabinet; engagement of permanent secretaries (career staff) alongside ministers (political appointees). |
| **Tier 1 ministry de-prioritises engagement** (operational crisis, competing priorities) | High | High | Quick wins early; visible programme value; sponsor escalation. |
| **Engagement fatigue in Tier 1 ministries** | Medium | Medium | Cap weekly working session at 90 minutes; rotate facilitator load; consolidate documentation requests. |
| **Tier 2 ministry produces low-quality AS-IS documentation** | Medium | Medium | Embedded technical support; ERA-based assessment of capacity; readiness threshold for moving to ADAPT. |
| **Tier 3 ministries lose momentum during 18-month preparation** | High | Low | Monthly bulletin; community-of-practice rotation; visible Champion success stories. |
| **Communication overwhelm** (too many products) | Medium | Low | Standard product set (Section 6.3); ruthless calendar discipline. |
| **EA Board becomes a rubber stamp** | Low | High | Real decisions tabled at EA Board; Architects' Notes circulated 48 hours in advance; minutes published. |
| **Inter-ministerial conflict** (data ownership, capability boundaries) | High | Medium | Reference Architecture as objective basis for resolution; sponsor-level escalation path; dispensation process. |

## 8.2 Risk register

The full risk register is maintained in TK-19 (Toolkit) and refreshed at each EA Board meeting.

# 9. SUCCESS METRICS

## 9.1 Engagement metrics

| Metric | Target | Measured by |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| Tier 1 attendance at weekly working sessions | ≥ 80% | Attendance log |
| Tier 1 sponsor attendance at Steering Committee | ≥ 75% | Attendance log |
| Stakeholder Commitment Letter signing rate (Tier 1) | 100% by end of Week 4 | Signed letters |
| EA Board attendance | ≥ 80% Tier 1 + Tier 2 | Attendance log |
| Tier 2 monthly progress report submission | 100% | Submitted reports |
| Tier 3 monthly bulletin readership | ≥ 60% | Email open rates / repository access |
| Community-of-practice participation | ≥ 40% Tier 3 ministries per session | Attendance log |
| Annual EA Conference attendance | ≥ 100 participants | Registration |

## 9.2 Outcome metrics

Engagement is a means, not an end. Outcome metrics measure whether engagement is producing the architectural results:

| Outcome | Target | Measured by |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| Champion ministries with completed AS-IS documentation | 100% | Method Guide deliverables checklist |
| Champion ministries with approved TO-BE Architecture | 100% | EA Board approval record |
| Wave 1 implementation initiatives launched | All planned Wave 1 | Programme dashboard |
| Architectural compliance rate (new projects) | ≥ 80% | EA Board compliance assessment |
| Number of Tier 3 ministries ready to move to Tier 1/2 in follow-on programme | ≥ 50% | ERA refresh at month 18 |
